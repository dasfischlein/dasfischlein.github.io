Ancients Midterm Study Guide:

Midterm Exam Study Questions:  History of Ancient Philosophy

Note:  you must bring your own blue books for the exam.

1.  Thales claimed that everything is water.  Explain what it means to be an eliminativist about every material entity except water.  Explain what it means to be a reductivist about every material entity except water.  Explain what it means to think everything supervenes on water.  Which view(s) entail that the sentence “this pen exists” false?  Which view(s) entail that the sentence “God exists” false?  Which view(s) entail that everything is made of water?
So the form of your answer should be:
            i. Explanation of Eliminativism about every material entity except water.
            ii. Explanation of Reductivism about every material entity except water.
            iii. Explanation of the claim that every material entity supervenes on water.
            iv. Statement of which view(s) entail that the sentence “this pen exists” is                              false.
            v.  Statement of which view(s) entail that the sentence “God exists” is false.
            vi.  Statement of which view(s) entail that everything is made of water.

2.  Formulate Leibniz’s Identity of Indiscernibles.  Formulate Leibniz’s Indiscernibility of Identicals.  Say what it is for x and y to be qualitatively identical. Give an example of qualitative identity.  Describe the Max Black Spheres Case, and say what it is attempting to show.
            i. Formulation of Leibniz’s Identity of Indiscernibles.
            ii. Formulation of Leibniz’s Indiscernibility of Identicals.
            iii. Complete this:  x is qualitatively identical to y =df __________________.
            iv. Example of qualitative identity.
            v.  Description of the Max Black Spheres case.
            vi.  Discussion of what the case is attempting to show.

3.  Formulate Pluralism.  Present and explain the Pro-Pluralism argument.  Formulate The Unity of Opposites.  Discuss how Heraclitus used this as an objection to the Pro-Pluralism Argument.  State something Heraclitus took to be an example that supports the Unity of Opposites.
            i. Formulation of Pluralism.
            ii. Basic idea behind the Pro-Pluralism Argument, in your own words.
            iii. The Pro-Pluralism Argument, in premise/conclusion form.
            iv. Justifications for each of the premises in the Pro-Pluralism Argument.
            v.  Formulation of the Unity of Opposites.
            vi.  Description of how Heraclitus used (v) to object to the Pro-Pluralism                                 Argument.
            vii.  An example that Heraclitus took to support the Unity of Opposites.

4.  Formulate Monism.  Present and explain the Must-Have Monism Argument.  Say whether the argument is valid, and explain why or why not.  Describe what you take to be the strongest objection to the Must-Have Monism argument.
            i. Formulation of Monism.
            ii. The basic idea behind the Must-Have Monism Argument, in your own                                 words.
            iii. The Must-Have Monism Argument, in premise/conclusion form.
            iv. Justifications for each premise of the Must-Have Monism argument.
            v.  Is the argument valid?
            vi.  Why or why not?
            vii.  Describe what you take to be the strongest objection to the Must-Have                            Monism Argument.

5.  Formulate the principle of Ontological Parsimony.  Describe how Bertrand Russell attempted to claim that theories that are more ontologically parsimonious are more likely to be true:  (i) give the examples I presented in class of the pair of theories, one of which is more parsimonious, and (ii) explain why Russell thought one would be more likely to be true in this case.  (Note:  there are things we should say in response to Russell’s defense of Ontological Parsimony, but I won’t ask you about them.)
            i. Formulation of the principle of Ontological Parsimony.
            ii. Description of the pair of theories I presented in class, related to this.
            iii. Explanation of why Russell thought that the more parsimonious of the two                       theories would have been more likely to be true.

In preparing for the short-answer section of the exam, you should know:

  • what it is for an argument to be valid
  • what it is for an argument to be sound
  • how to translate claims into their logical form

I also recommend looking through the arguments we discussed when covering Logic.  You should be able to recognize when arguments of that sort are valid and when they’re invalid.

Finally, an important note:  prepare for every one of the essay questions.  It’s not uncommon for students to tell me, “I studied for every question except the one you asked!”  Don’t take chances.  Any one (or two) of these questions, or combinations of pieces of several of them, could be on the exam.

Also, if you are wondering about how I’m using the terms “present”, “evaluate”, “formulate”, and “explain”, look on the section of the syllabus under For Your Reference.  That section of the syllabus will also be given to you on exam day.  You will not be allowed to use notes (obviously).
